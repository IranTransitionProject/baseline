# Iran Is Not Iraq

## And Anyone Who Plans as if It Is Will Create Something Worse

**By Hooman Mehr | [hooman@mac.com](mailto:hooman@mac.com) | Brief \#3 | [Iran Transition Project](https://hmehr.substack.com/p/iran-the-convergence-briefs) | February 2026**

---

## The Argument That Freezes Everything

Every serious conversation about Iran's future hits the same wall within five minutes: "But look what happened in Iraq."

The argument works because it is emotionally powerful and partially correct. Iraq's post-2003 transition was a catastrophe. De-Ba'athification dissolved security forces and civil service, producing the insurgent recruitment pool that fueled a decade of war. The Islamic State emerged from the wreckage. Hundreds of thousands died.

This is not in dispute. What is in dispute is whether Iraq's failure generalizes to Iran.

The regime uses this as its strongest card. Khamenei has explicitly warned that without the Islamic Republic, Iran would become "another Syria, another Iraq, another Libya." State media amplifies post-Saddam chaos whenever protests threaten stability.

But analytical paralysis is itself a policy choice \-- one that defaults to the status quo. And the status quo in February 2026 is a regime that has killed thousands of its own citizens, a population that has risen in all 31 provinces, an economy in structural collapse, and a nuclear program at weapons-relevant enrichment. The question "what happens next" is happening whether anyone plans for it or not.

## Seven Structural Differences That Actually Matter

**1\. Iran Has a State. Iraq Did Not.** When the Ba'ath party fell, Iraq's state fell with it. Ba'athist Iraq had fused party and state so completely that removing the party meant removing governance itself. Iran is different. The Islamic Republic is layered on top of pre-existing state institutions \-- a centralized bureaucracy dating to the Safavid era, a modern civil service built during the Pahlavi period. The IRGC has penetrated these institutions deeply, but penetration is not replacement. Iran's Health Ministry, road system, utilities, and universities function. Its Artesh maintains institutional identity separate from the IRGC. A transition does not start from zero.

**2\. Iran Has National Identity. Iraq Was a Colonial Construction.** Iraq was drawn on a map by Sykes-Picot and assembled from three Ottoman provinces with no historical unity. When the state fell, sectarian and ethnic identities filled the vacuum. Iran has been a continuously identifiable political entity for over 2,500 years. Persian identity predates Islam by a millennium. Iranians across ethnic lines \-- Persian, Azeri, Kurdish, Baloch, Arab, Lur \-- share a civilizational identity that Iraqi groups never had. This does not eliminate ethnic tensions, but the baseline cohesion is incomparably stronger.

**3\. Iran's Diaspora Is a Strategic Asset. Iraq's Was Not.** Iraq's diaspora in 2003 was small and dominated by figures like Chalabi with no internal legitimacy. Iran's 4-5 million diaspora is one of the most educated on earth: engineers, physicians, technologists, lawyers, academics. In February 2026, it mobilized at unprecedented scale \-- 250,000 in Munich, 350,000 each in Toronto and Los Angeles in a single day. The diaspora cannot and should not govern Iran. But it represents a reservoir of expertise, institutional knowledge, and financial capacity that no previous transition case has had.

**4\. Iran's Military Is Divided. Iraq's Was Not.** Iraq had one military fused with the Ba'ath party. Iran has two: the Artesh (\~420,000) with professional military culture and constitutional border defense mission, and the IRGC (\~190,000) as the regime's praetorian guard. This duality creates a transition pathway Iraq never had \-- the Artesh as stabilizing force while IRGC elements are managed through tiered absorption.

**5\. Iran's Economy Has Structure. Iraq's Was a Rentier Shell.** Iraq in 2003 was a pure rentier state \-- oil revenue and nothing else. Iran has domestic manufacturing (automotive, steel, petrochemicals), a skilled labor force, a technology sector, agricultural production, and a merchant class with centuries of institutional memory. GDP structure: \~40% services, \~35% industry, \~12% agriculture. The private sector exists. Sanctions relief would produce immediate economic stimulus that no post-conflict case has had.

**6\. Iran Has Not Been Invaded. Iraq Was.** Foreign occupation was the single most delegitimizing factor in Iraq's transition. If Iran's transition comes through internal dynamics, it will not carry this burden. A government formed by Iranians starts with legitimacy that Iraq's Coalition Provisional Authority never had. This is the strongest practical argument against military-imposed regime change: not that Iranians don't deserve freedom, but that externally imposed transitions cannot produce it.

**7\. The Failure Modes Are Known. In 2003, They Were Not.** In 2003, the specific mechanisms by which transition destroys countries had not been documented. In 2026, we have four detailed case studies \-- Iraq, Libya, Syria, Afghanistan \-- each with named failure modes. We know that dissolving security forces produces insurgencies, that sectarian quotas produce competitive exclusion, that economic collapse produces warlordism. None of this knowledge helps if nobody applies it. But it exists.

## Where the Comparison IS Valid

The IRGC problem is real. The IRGC parallel society enrolls 2-10 million people. Dissolving it overnight would create de-Ba'athification on a larger scale. But its internal hollowness \-- dependence on patronage rather than ideology \-- makes absorption possible in a way that was not available for Iraq's Sunni officer class.

Ethnic fragmentation risk is real. Kurdish, Baloch, Arab, and Azeri populations have legitimate grievances. But Iran's civilizational national identity provides a counter-gravity that Iraq lacked.

Economic collapse risk is real. But sanctions relief provides a stimulus tool Iraq did not have, and the diaspora provides reconstruction capacity Iraq's diaspora could not offer.

External interference risk is real. Russia, China, Turkey, Saudi Arabia, Israel, and the U.S. all have interests that may conflict with Iranian self-determination. But Iran's population is far more sophisticated about foreign interference than Iraq's was in 2003\.

## What Actually Follows from This

First, the regime's "us or chaos" argument is a false binary. The honest version is: "transition is dangerous and must be carefully managed." The regime's version is: "transition is impossible, therefore accept us forever." These are not the same claim.

Second, transition planning is not optional. Whether transition comes through uprising, negotiation, elite fracture, or external pressure, structural conditions make the status quo untenable. The ICG, Atlantic Council, and Stimson Center have all concluded this. Planning is not advocacy for regime change. It is responsible contingency work.

Third, the planning must be Iranian. The clearest lesson from Iraq, Libya, Syria, and Afghanistan: externally imposed governance fails. Iran's framework must be designed by Iranians, informed by the diaspora's expertise, tested against historical failure modes, and implemented by institutions inside Iran. This is not a Western project with Iranian input. It is an Iranian project.
