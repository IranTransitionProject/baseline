### Observation 002: Iran's Structural Advantages Are Real (v1.0)
* **Diagnosis:** In every negative comparison case (Iraq, Libya, Syria, Afghanistan), at least two of the following were absent: strong national identity, educated population, institutional bureaucratic depth, energy resource base. Iran has all four. This does not guarantee success -- but it means the failure modes of the comparison cases are not inevitable.

* **Strategic implication:** The chaos narrative ("Iran will become the next Iraq/Libya") is analytically incomplete. Iran's structural advantages make managed transition possible in a way it was not in the comparison cases. The framework's job is to demonstrate this with specificity, not to promise it.
* **ITB anchors:** D2.1, D4, C4, E5

### Observation 005: Colonial-Border Comparisons Are Analytically Misleading (v1.0)
* **Diagnosis:** The default comparison set (Iraq, Libya, Syria, Afghanistan) shares a feature Iran does not: colonial-era borders that do not correspond to historical civilizational or national identity. Iran's borders correspond to a 2,500-year-old civilizational footprint. Iran absorbed its invaders culturally rather than being absorbed by them -- a pattern unique among Middle Eastern states. Using colonial-border states as whole-case comparators systematically overstates Iran's territorial fragmentation risk and understates its institutional resilience.

* **Strategic implication:** The ISA historical case study framework has been restructured into three tracks: (A) mechanism extraction from colonial-border states (specific portable lessons), (B) civilizational-state transitions (Turkey, Japan, Spain, Indonesia, South Africa) for territorial/identity/governance dimensions, and (C) theological authority transitions (no clean modern parallel -- Turkey 1924 is closest). The regime's "chaos without us" narrative gains its power from the Iraq/Libya analogy. Demonstrating why that analogy is structurally flawed is itself a strategic contribution of this framework.

### Observation 010: The Two Clocks Problem -- The Deal's Temporal Vulnerability (v1.1)
* **Diagnosis:** The deal track operates under two independent clocks that interact destructively. The Succession Clock (months to years) governs Khamenei's decline and the eschatological faction's institutional capture trajectory. The Washington Patience Clock (weeks to months) governs the finite window for US-Iran diplomacy. The shorter clock wins. The velocity gap: deal negotiations move faster than institutional capture, creating a window where a deal can be signed. But the deal's most vulnerable period is post-signing. The wrong interlocutor thesis: Washington is negotiating with Segment 3 (genuine pragmatists, ~15-25% of institutional power) while deal survival depends on overcoming Segments 1+2 (~45-65% of institutional power). The negotiating counterparts lack the institutional weight to deliver what they are promising.

* **Strategic implication:** Deal assessment must include a temporal stress test: "If this deal is signed today, what is the probability it survives (a) the next Supreme Leader selection, (b) the next US political cycle, and (c) simultaneous eschatological sabotage and institutional friction?" If the answer to any is below 50%, the deal's expected value for the population is negative.
